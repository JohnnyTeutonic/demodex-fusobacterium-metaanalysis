BIOINFORMATICS OVERVIEW -- DEMODEX / ROSACEA MOLECULAR MITE-BURDEN PROBE
========================================================================
(High-level internal summary of the computational work to date. Not an email,
not a manuscript -- a plain-language overview to reuse when describing what the
bioinformatics arm did and found.)


THE QUESTION
------------
Demodex mites are strongly over-represented in rosacea, and a sensible clinical
idea is to measure the mite burden and then scale antiparasitic treatment to it.
The reference way to measure burden (standardised skin-surface biopsy + mite
count) is slow and operator-dependent. So we asked a purely computational
question: can the relative abundance of the bacteria that ride with the mite,
read straight out of ordinary 16S microbiome data, act as a cheap molecular
proxy for mite burden?


WHAT WE BUILT (all public data, pure-Python, fully reproducible)
----------------------------------------------------------------
1. AUDIT. Queried NCBI for every rosacea/Demodex sequencing dataset: 656 runs
   across 25 BioProjects, 17 of them genuinely on-topic. Two became the spine of
   the analysis:
     - an ivermectin (antiparasitic) before/after facial-skin 16S series, and
     - a Demodex-infected-vs-control 16S series.

2. MARKER PANEL. Took the bacteria the literature ties to the mite -- chiefly
   Snodgrassella alvi (a documented Demodex "core" microbe), plus Bacillus
   oleronius, Cutibacterium kroppenstedtii and Bartonella quintana -- fetched
   their 16S reference sequences, and reduced each to a set of "diagnostic"
   DNA words (31-mers) that are unique to that organism AND absent from a panel
   of 24 common skin bacteria. That background-subtraction step is what makes
   the markers specific rather than picking up conserved regions shared with
   ordinary skin flora.

3. INDEX. For every sample we streamed the raw reads, counted how many carried
   the diagnostic words for each marker, and combined the Demodex-associated
   markers into a single "mite index".

4. TESTS. (a) Does the index drop after ivermectin? (b) Does it separate
   Demodex-infected from control samples?


WHAT WE FOUND (reported straight, including the negatives)
----------------------------------------------------------
- SPECIFICITY HAS TO BE ENGINEERED. The first naive index was nonsense (one rare
  mite-bacterium appeared to match ~half of all reads) until we subtracted a
  background of common flora and required two diagnostic words per read. And the
  background has to MATCH THE BODY SITE: a skin-only background left the
  eye-surface samples open to cross-matching. Lesson: closed-reference marker
  counting on short reads is only as good as its negative control.

- THE TREATMENT TEST IS NULL. After ivermectin the index did not move in any
  consistent way (no statistical signal). We read this as sampling physics:
  surface swabs barely reach the follicle where the mite lives, and killing
  mites can release their bacteria onto the surface -- so a surface readout is a
  poor instrument for a follicular load.

- THE GROUP TEST WORKS, ONCE THE CONTROL IS RIGHT. With a skin-only background
  the composite failed to separate Demodex-infected from control (and one marker,
  Bartonella, even pointed the wrong way -- it was cross-matching eye-surface
  bacteria). After we matched the background to the body site (adding ocular and
  related environmental organisms), that artefact collapsed and the composite
  DID separate infected from control in the right direction (a respectable,
  borderline-significant group difference). The signal is carried by
  Snodgrassella alvi (the best-established Demodex bacterium) and Bacillus.

- METHOD NOW CHARACTERISED. A held-out control shows the index detects its own
  targets well (74-98%) and is clean against distant relatives, but it resolves
  only to about GENUS level -- close cousins cross-react (e.g. it can't tell
  Bartonella quintana from B. henselae). So it is a genus-level screen, not a
  species-level assay -- now measured, not assumed.


WHY THIS IS USEFUL (the honest bottom line)
-------------------------------------------
- A surface microbiome composite is informative only with a body-site-matched
  control, and even then it gives a coarse GROUP-LEVEL signal, not a per-patient
  mite count. So for grading an individual's treatment you should still measure
  the mite directly; the microbiome index is at most a population-level adjunct.
- The single most defensible molecular lead is a curated Snodgrassella alvi
  assay -- ideally from sampling that actually reaches the follicle (which
  surface swabs miss), with reference/background sets matched to the body site.
  Bartonella should be dropped (it can't be told from its congener by 16S).


KNOWN LIMITATIONS (now bounded, not open-ended)
-----------------------------------------------
- The "DNA-word" method resolves to ~genus level (now quantified); species-level
  resolution would need ASV/oligotyping (DADA2) -- the stated upgrade path.
- The treatment cohort is small; the validation cohort is from the eye surface,
  a different niche from facial rosacea. A facial cohort with follicle
  (cyanoacrylate "glue") sampling exists (PRJEB82826, 96 samples), but its
  public records carry no infestation/clinical labels, so a labelled facial
  validation is blocked until we obtain the authors' sample key.
- The index is always a relative proxy, never an actual mite census.


REPRODUCIBILITY
---------------
Everything is scripted in pure Python with offline self-tests; all network calls
are read-only and cached; every dataset accession, intermediate table and marker
panel is saved so the numbers regenerate from scratch. See README.md and the
results/ write-ups (E0 audit, E3 treatment, E4 validation, E5 method
characterisation) for specifics, and demodex_surrogate_16S.tex for the
manuscript draft.
